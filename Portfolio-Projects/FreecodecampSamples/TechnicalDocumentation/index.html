Overview

Philippine folklore creatures are figures from oral tradition that blend pre‑colonial beliefs, regional languages, and later influences. Names and traits vary by province, but the stories often serve as cultural lessons about respect for nature, community safety, and personal conduct.

This document provides quick reference notes for widely mentioned beings. Each section summarizes: a brief description, typical abilities or behaviors, customary protections or countermeasures, and a practical field/safety note distilled from folk guidance. It is not exhaustive scholarship—just a concise guide for orientation.

Use the sidebar to jump between entries. Section IDs match the sidebar labels; smooth scrolling keeps context clear. Where terms differ across regions, the entries favor commonly recognized variants and note alternatives when helpful.

Tikbalang

The Tikbalang is a tall, sinewy creature with the head of a horse and long limbs. It is known for misleading travelers along forest paths and mountain trails.

Etiquette and respect are emphasized in folk advice: ask permission when passing through the woods and avoid disturbing old trees or strange cairns.

  • Abilities: Illusion, misdirection, incredible speed.
  • Weaknesses: Wearing your shirt inside-out, polite greetings, and keeping calm are said to break its tricks.
Field note: If you feel you are going in circles, pause, reverse your shirt, and say “tabi tabi po.”
Kapre

The Kapre is a tree-dwelling giant, often seen perched on a large branch smoking a cigar. Despite its fearsome size, stories vary from mischievous to quietly watchful.

Encounters typically occur near ancient balete (banyan) trees or large mango trees at dusk.

  • Abilities: Invisibility to chosen observers, tree-bound habitation, intimidation.
  • Weaknesses: Daylight and respectful offerings are said to appease or discourage attention.
Observation: Children reported lights and smoke above the canopy; no fires found on inspection.
Aswang

The Aswang is a shapeshifting predator with many regional forms—sometimes appearing as a person by day and transforming at night.

Common motifs include a long, proboscis-like tongue and an aversion to strong scents and sacred objects.

  • Abilities: Shapeshifting, stealth, heightened senses.
  • Weaknesses: Garlic, salt, blessed items, and the stingray tail (buntot pagi) in some traditions.
Procedure: Maintain perimeter protections; coordinate watch rotations on moonless nights.
Duwende

Duwende are small earth spirits associated with mounds, gardens, and anthills. They can bring luck or misfortune depending on how they are treated.

Proper conduct includes asking permission when stepping near earthen mounds and avoiding wanton disturbance of their homes.

  • Abilities: Blessings or pranks, home guardianship, invisibility.
  • Weaknesses: Disrespect invites trouble; courtesy and offerings restore favor.
Custom: Say “tabi tabi po” when passing by suspected dwellings to signal respect.
Diwata

Diwata are nature spirits linked to forests, rivers, and mountains. They are often portrayed as benevolent guardians who reward kindness and punish greed.

Rituals of thanksgiving and careful stewardship of the land are recurring themes in stories about Diwata.

  • Abilities: Blessings, healing, guardianship of natural places.
  • Weaknesses: Desecration of sacred sites provokes withdrawal or retribution rather than a literal weak point.
Guideline: Leave no trace; offer thanks when taking from the forest or river.
White Lady

The White Lady is a spectral apparition in a white dress, associated with accidents along dark roads and bridges.

Folktales frame the spirit as a victim seeking justice or acknowledgment, appearing unexpectedly to motorists and pedestrians.

  • Abilities: Sudden manifestation, fear induction, localized temperature drops.
  • Weaknesses: Prayers, calm focus, and avoiding reckless driving in known hotspots.
Safety note: Reduce speed on reported stretches; keep high beams and dash cams active when possible.
Manananggal

The Manananggal can split its upper body from its lower half and is often depicted as a winged woman who hunts at night.

Accounts say it targets pregnant women, extending a long, thread‑like tongue to feed. Villagers protect homes with garlic and salt.

  • Abilities: Flight, dismemberment, bloodsucking.
  • Weaknesses: Salt/garlic on the lower torso prevents reattachment before dawn.
Field note: Secure rooftops and windows; inspect for corrosive residue near dawn.
Tiyanak

The Tiyanak mimics an infant’s cry to lure would‑be rescuers. Once approached, it reveals its monstrous form.

Many stories portray it as a restless child spirit, with variations across regions in the Philippines.

  • Abilities: Vocal mimicry, shape‑shifting, surprising strength.
  • Weaknesses: Rice or salt scattered along its path compels it to stop and count.
Incident: Hiker diverted off trail; rice scattering provided time to escape.
Wak Wak

The Wak Wak is a bird‑like creature whose name imitates the beating of wings and its eerie nighttime call.

Sightings frequently overlap with Manananggal reports, suggesting either shared behaviors or misclassification.

  • Abilities: Nocturnal flight, sharp claws, fear‑inducing shriek.
  • Weaknesses: Continuous loud noise (bells, whistles) disrupts hunting patterns.
Mitigation: Coordinate bell‑ringing watches during peak migration periods.
Sigbin

The Sigbin reportedly walks backward with its head lowered, draining blood and inducing sudden weakness.

Legends sometimes describe domestication by occult practitioners, though details vary by province.

  • Abilities: Stealth, bloodsucking, paralysis.
  • Weaknesses: Garlic and sacred symbols are customary repellents.
Case note: Victims showed minimal struggle; timing clustered around new moon.
Pugot

The Pugot appears headless and is associated with possession or control of corpses, inspiring fear and confusion.

Reports concentrate near burial grounds and abandoned structures bordering farmland.

  • Abilities: Corpse control, psychological terror.
  • Weaknesses: Holy water, prayers, and ritual protections.
Observation: Headless silhouette preceded unexplained movement within a closed crypt.
Mangkukulam

The Mangkukulam is a practitioner of sorcery blamed for curses, illness, and misfortune.

Folktales emphasize indirect harm—strings of accidents or lingering sickness—rather than open confrontation.

  • Abilities: Spellcasting, curses, limited weather manipulation.
  • Weaknesses: Counter‑rituals and reversals; antidotes mentioned in oral tradition.
Procedure: Identify suspected vector; consult healer; perform reversal before next moon phase.
